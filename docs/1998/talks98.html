Here is the timetable for the Summer Scholarship Programme talks for 1998. Please click on the title of a talk for further details of the project.

Monday 31st August

  • Elson Mourao (University of Coimbra, Portugal) - A Middleware Environment for Parallel Java on the Hitachi SR2201
  • Scott Trudeau (Oakland University, USA) - JavaGene: Java Middleware to Access Distributed Molecular Biology Database
  • Krzysztof Zelazowski (Warsaw University of Technology, Poland) - Benchmarking and related work on the Hitachi SR2201

Tuesday 1st September

  • Tom Doel (University of Edinburgh, UK) - Java Simulation of MPI Collective Communications
  • Mario Mitas (Slovak Technical University, Slovakia) - EPIC-2001: Extending EPIC to the Windows Desktop

Wednesday 2nd September

  • Sam Carr (University of Oxford, UK) - Crystal Structure Determination from Powder Diffraction
  • Ruth Durie (University of Edinburgh, UK) - VisualGA: Visualisation of a Genetic Algorithm

Thursday 3rd September - NB: These talks will be held in Room 5210 of JCMB.

  • Giampietro Lea (University of Wales at Swansea, UK) - Parallel Short-ranged Molecular Dynamics Simulation with HPF
  • Eurico Moita (University of Lisbon, Portugal) - Parallel vector-topological data input for Geographical Information Systems
  • Stephan Zeipel (University of Hanover, Germany) - 3D modelling of heat flow in sedimentary basins - the impact of rifted basement topography

Friday 4th September

  • Amit Asaravala (University of California, San Diego, USA) - Intersim Java: Extending the Network Simulator
  • Adam Carter (University of Edinburgh, UK) - Application of BICG Method to Large Satellite Magnetic Datasets
  • John Lin (University of California, San Diego, USA) - WebDB: Bringing Structure to the WWW

Please note: All sessions begin at 1pm, and will be held in Room 6301 of the James Clerk Maxwell Building, with the exception of the talks held on Thursday which will be held in Room 5210.

At a time to be arranged during the week beginning Monday 7th September:

  • Dave Oldham (University of Edinburgh, UK) - "What makes a good Webpage?"
